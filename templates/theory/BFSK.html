BFSK Modulation

Binary frequency-shift keying (BFSK), in which the carrier amplitude and carrier phase are both maintained constant, while the carrier frequency is keyed between the two possible values used to represent symbols 0 and 1.

In binary frequency-shift keying (BFSK), symbols 0 and 1 are distinguished from each other by transmitting one of two sinusoidal waves that differ in frequency by a fixed amount.

b(t) =    {

√2*Eb/√Tbcos(2πf1t)  for symbol 1 corresponding to i = 1
√2*Eb/√Tbcos(2πf2t)  for symbol 0 corresponding to i = 2

}

Then, multiplying b(t) by the sinusoidal carrier wave of above Equation with phase φc set equal to zero for convenience of presentation, we get the BASK wave where Eb is the transmitted signal energy per bit.

When the frequencies f1and f2 are chosen in such a way that they differ from each other by an amount equal to the reciprocal of the bit duration the BFSK signal is referred to as Sunde's BFSK after its originator.

This modulated signal is a continuous-phase signal in the sense that phase continuity is always maintained, including the inter-bit switching times.

Binary data stream:

A binary data stream is a sequence of bits that represent digital information, such as text, images, audio, or video. Each bit is a binary digit that can have one of two possible values: 0 or 1. The bits are transmitted or stored in a sequential order, with each bit representing a unit of information.

Carrier wave:

A carrier wave is a high-frequency signal that is modulated to carry information in a communication system. The carrier wave does not carry any information by itself but provides a stable platform for the modulation process. The frequency and amplitude of the carrier wave remain constant while the information is superimposed on it. The carrier wave is typically a sine wave, and its frequency determines the bandwidth of the communication channel.

Amplitude-shift keying (ASK):

Amplitude-shift keying is a digital modulation technique that uses the amplitude of a carrier wave to represent the binary data. In ASK, the amplitude of the carrier wave is switched between two different levels to represent 0 and 1 in the binary data stream. For example, if the carrier wave has an amplitude of A and A/2, it can represent 0 and 1, respectively. The receiver detects the amplitude changes in the carrier wave and decodes them back into the original binary data. ASK is a simple and efficient modulation technique but is sensitive to noise and distortion in the communication channel.

Non-Coherent Detection of BFSK Signal

  1. In BFSK, transmissions of symbols 1 and 0 are represented by two carrier waves of frequencies f1 and f2, respectively.
  2. The receiver consists of two paths, one dealing with frequency f1 (i.e., symbol 1) and the other dealing with frequency f2 (i.e., symbol 0)
  3. Path 1 uses a band-pass filter of mid-band frequency f1. The filtered version of the incoming BFSK signal is envelope-detected and then sampled at time t=iTb to produce the output v1.
  4. Path 2 uses a band-pass filter of mid-band frequency f2. As with path 2, filtered version of the BFSK signal is envelope detected and then sampled at time t=iTb to produce a different output v2.
  5. The outputs of the two paths, v1 and v2 are applied to a comparator, where decisions on the composition of the BFSK signal are repeated every seconds.
